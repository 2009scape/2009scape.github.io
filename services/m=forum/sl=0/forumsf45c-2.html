The idea I'm suggesting here is a weekly Dungoneering Distraction and Diversion. THE OVERALL TOPIC IS LONG, BUT THE SECOND POST CONTAINS ONE PARAGRAPH CALLED SUMMARY THAT GIVES YOU ALL MAJOR DETAILS IN A FEW WORDS. More specifics can be found in the rest of the post

This first post is just explaining why I think this minigame is important; to go onto the summary and details of the idea, just skip to the next post.

I will start by saying that I rather like the Dungeoneering skill overall, but I find three major flaws with the skill that were not originally intended when it was designed, I believe.

1) Dungeoneering was designed to attempt to have people spend at least some time using their skills to make gear and such for their team. In practice, this does not happen. With good groups, making gear is simply a waste of time and thus these skills are never used.

2) Other than minimally important bonus rooms, , all doors are tailored to the levels of people in the run. For example, in a solo run, if your firemaking is 50, then the firemaking doors are 50; if you're 95, then they're 95. This rather turns skills required to open doors into "invisible" skills; it doesn't matter what your level is, you (or someone in the party) has the level. Thus, while the reason this HAS to be true is understandable, skill levels are irrelevant for skill doors.

3) Lastly, once you hit 69, your Dungeoneering level basically does not even matter anymore. There is no progression of the skill other than the ability to bind new items at extremely high levels.


The minigame I am proposing, I think you will find, strongly combats all three of these issues.

In the sands near Nardah, the town elders have recently grown inquisitive as to what powers lie dormant in the nearby Pyramid of Peril, the resting site of one of their greatest leaders. Having recently heard of the exploits of adventurers into the depths of Daemonheim, the town elders wish to perform exploration into this pyramid.


Summary:
The idea behind this dungeoneering minigame is that over the course of a week, you'll be able to perform a one-time exploration of the Pyramid of Peril. Unlike normal dungeoneering floors, the skill levels required to open doors will not be based on your level but get harder with each floor; thus, you will be able to explore into the depths of the Pyramid of Peril until the door levels are too high for you. However, your Dungeoneering level will grant you a limited number of skill level boosts to compensate for low skills, making your Dungeoneering level relevant, even if you're beyond 69. Gear is not lost as you move from floor to floor, making spending time to craft gear more useful; however, lower floors also have lower materials, so you won't leave the first floor in full Promethium. Weekly rewards will be granted based on how far you explored that week and are generally better than Daemonheim.


Details:
The idea behind this minigame, like many other of the Distractions and Diversions, is to break the monotony of doing the same dungeoneering routine over and over and over; however, at the same time, you can only run the Pyramid of Peril once per week. The Pyramid of Peril is a solo-only dungeon exercise where you begin on the first floor and work your way up. The floors are roughly comparable to a medium-sized dungeon in terms of size. There is no binding items in the Pyramid of Peril; when you start on floor 1, you have no items whatsoever bound. Upon completing a floor, any items you have are carried to the next floor. If you have a familiar summoned, you will receive the pouch for that familiar.

Skill requirements in the Pyramid of Peril are completely unrelated to your level. This is unlike Daemonheim where the doors along the required path of the floor are always accomplishable by the skill level of one person in the run. On the first floor, the skill level required to get through rooms is roughly 5. Combat skills to kill the monsters are slightly higher, but still low. However, each floor requires higher and higher skills. On average, the skill required on the next floor is roughly 5 higher than the previous floor, such that floor 10 requires skills around approximately 50 to complete the floor.

The player has access to a resource called Dungeon Points (DP). The player's DP is equal to their Dungeoneering level; thus, no matter how high their Dungoneering level is, they will always receive rewards from that level. A player may spend 1 DP in order to increase any skill of theirs permanently by 1. This is a permanent increase for the entire week inside Pyramid of Peril. This also allows a player to work around a skill they are weak in. For example, if a certain player has a Runecrafting level of 30 but every other skill is roughly 70, they could spend three DP to increase their Runecrafting level to 33 so that they can open a door, craft runes, etc. However, if the player completes the floor, the increase will be permanent for the remainder of the week (only inside Pyramid of Peril of course); on all further floors, they will have 33 Runecrafting but will have spent 3 DP. Of course, a player could also choose to use this, for example, to increase their Strength to 150 early in the run and kill monsters quicker, but this will restrict their ability to open doors later in the run, and there is a limited amount of DP to be spread amongst all skills.

There are no experience modifiers in Pyramid of Peril. Rather, at the end of the week (or when the player speaks to the npc's outside), they will receive experience based on how many rooms were opened and how many floors were cleared. Bosses in Pyramid of Peril cannot be encountered until all rooms on a floor are opened. Thus, there are no "optional" doors in Pyramid of Peril, so if you cannot open a single door on a floor, you will not be able to progress past that floor. However, you will still receive partial credit based on what percentage of the floor you were able to clear, though you will not be able to go beyond that floor. Every time a player dies in Pyramid of Peril, they lose one DP; this is in order to make up for the fact that there is no experience penalty. The penalty if relatively minor for a single death, but over many floors could compound to be serious if you die too many times as your DP is largely what allows you to open doors beyond your levels. Furthermore, if your DP become negative (which can only occur through dying with zero DP), then you will be unable to open any additional doors on the floor, effectively stopping your progress at that point.

Pyramid of Peril is not something that has to be done all at once. Everytime you complete a floor, you progress is saved, and you can return at any time in the week in order to continue further floors. If you leave partway through a floor, the number of rooms you cleared will be saved in terms of measuring experience, but the floor itself will not be saved. Upon trying to re-enter the floor, you will be allowed to continue, but the floor will be reset. Thus, for example, let us say you clear floors 1-10 then leave partway through floor 11. Upon returning, you will find floor 11 to be completely reset to its original state (any DP you spent/lost will be returned to what it was when you originally had started the floor, including any items you obtained).

Each week, the floors are randomly generated but are the same for every person. This is somewhat similar to the idea of the Penguin Hunting minigame where each week the penguins spawn randomly, but the spawn location is the same for every person on every server. This allows you, if you choose, to partially run a floor in order to find out what exists on the floor then reset and re-run it with this knowledge; however, of course, in the process, you are effectively doubling the amount of time required to run these floors. Another side effect of this is that the skill levels required to clear a floor do not change throughout the week. Thus, if you choose to do so, if you find yourself confronted with a door you cannot yet open, you can train that skill during the week and come back with a higher skill in order to open it. Because the floors are the same, no person will have an unfair advantage over another (i.e. one person's floor requiring 95 Fletching whereas another required 95 construction).

Because these are completely solo runs, any skills required to complete the run must be entirely your own. Another side effect of this is that having higher gathering/crafting professions can help you. For example, having a higher mining/smithing will allow you to make yourself better armor. This is of course true in Daemonheim as well; the difference here is that 1) no one else can make it for you, and 2) because all items are saved from floor to floor, items you make will stay with you. This provides more incentive to utilize your professions, unlike a normal Daemonheim floor where the time you spend on the floor is so short that it's not worth making gear. Recall, however, that lower level floors will have lower level materials, so you won't leave floor 1 with a full set of Promethium gear.

The rewards from Pyramid of Peril are somewhat similar to those from Daemonheim. You obtain rewards based on how many floors you cleared and, on your best floor, how many rooms you cleared. There is no upper floor to Pyramid of Peril; rather, you eventually reach a point where you can't go any further. Theoretically, this could be a floor with skill requirements of 150 or so when you factor in skill boosts from DP and and potions/familiars. The limit may be high, but it will be eventually reached. The rewards are somewhat similar to running Daemonheim without having ever reset; i.e. running Floor 1 gives roughly Floor 1 rewards + 1 floors of prestige, Floor 2 gives roughly Floor 2 rewards + 2 floors of prestige, and so forth. This turns the rewards into roughly a quadratic progression with floor, but of course this is added together for each floor.

The rewards are 2-fold. First, you obtain Dungeoneering experience, just like Daemonheim floors. Second, like Daemonheim, you obtain Pyramid of Peril tokens. Pyramid of Peril tokens can be exchanged for standard Dungeoneering tokens, but they can also be exchanged for experience in any skill. The conversion from tokens to experience in these skills is roughly comparable to that of a lamp; that is, X Pyramid of Peril tokens (whatever X may be) translates into experience equal to the level of the skill you're putting it into. This allows you to utilize Pyramid of Peril to train other skills to a degree as well should you choose to do so.

Additional notes: These are somewhat minor pieces of the idea but still potential sources of problems that were brought up I felt I should address.

1) It was noted that a person could level up a skill really high with DP then keep restarting the floor in order to get easy experience in that skill. There are a few possible ways to correct this. One option would be to simply not allow experience to be gained inside the pyramid and simply allow the rewards to handle experience gained. Another option would be to allow you to get experience on every floor, but if you restart the floor you get no more experience from it.

2) It was noted that the smuggler could be used to buy high level items on low levels floors so you have awesome gear for all floors with relatively little effort. First, note that Pyramid of Peril is NOT Daemonheim, so there is not necessarily a Smuggler in here. However, if there was, one option would be to make the Smuggler have limited stock. The stocks of all items start at zero, and every new floor causes a small amount to be added to the stock he had when you left the previous floor. However, stock is only added for items relevant to that floor, so for example, floor 2 does not add Promethium ores to the stock, but high level floors occasionally would. If this would implemented, you could also allow DP to be spent to add to the Smuggler's stock, giving another potential use for your Dungoneering level.
