Hello. I am writing this guide as a guide of understanding Polish, but not necessarily speaking Polish. It may be used as a general guide of speaking Polish, but it would be necessary to learn other aspects beforehand. Generally, I'll go going in-depth into basic topics.

To start off, to make this guide less confusing, I'll use some rules of what will denote what.
-When I will be writing a Polish word by giving the English pronunciation, I will put the word in [brackets], with the accented syllable CAPITALIZED.
-When I will be giving the definition of a Polish word by writing the definition in English, I will write the definition in (Parentheses) after the Polish word.
-When I will be writing comments, I will write them *in between asterisks*
-When I will denote Polish lettering, I will put ' after the letter.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In addition, if you need any help with a Polish phrase being translated, please feel free to post it here and I, or someone else, will gladly translate it.

In Polish, there are some differences between the spelling and the pronunciation of words, and words are sometimes pronounced differently when isolated, or in a word group.
We hear [skLEP] and we write sklep (store);
we hear [xLEP], but we write chleb (bread).
We hear [BUwka], we write bul'ka (bread roll);
we hear [uAFka], we write l'awka (bench);
we hear [dziECko], we write dziecko;
we hear [vzGUze], we write wzg�rze.
If a word ends in a consonant, this consonant will always be pronounced as voiceless (in isolated words). To summarize, in isolated words there will be no difference between the final, unaccented consonants of:
b and p,
d and t,
dz and c,
w and f,
g and k,
z and s, or
rz and sz.
As examples:
Arab (arab) and rap (rap) *The b will be pronounced like a p*,
dziad (grandfather) and brat (brother) *The d will be pronounced like a t*,
pienia'dz (coin; singular of money) and miesia'c (month),
staw (pond) and Staff (staff),
pocia'g (train) and Irak (Iraq),
raz (one; once) and czas (time), or
malarz (painter) and listonosz (mailman).

When a voiced and voiceless consonant meet in teh middle of a word or between two words, it is called assimilation, because the pronunciation must be uniform to ease the speaker. The first of the consonants undergoes assimilation. In the following examples, I will first write the isolated word, and then the word undergoind assimilation:
chleb (bread) [xLEp] - chleb z'ytni (rye bread) [xlebJITni],
brat (brother) [bRAt] - brat Basi (Basia's brother) [bradBASi].

Verbs:

The first, the most commonly used and the most useful verb is byc' [pronounced near-identically to a bad word in English] (to be). As with all other European languages, this verb is irregular.

The Present Tense:

1. Ja (I) jestem
2. Ty (You) jestes'
3. on/ona/ono (he/she/it) jest

4. My (We) jestes'my
5. Wy (You, plural) jestes'cie
6. Oni/one (They) sa'

1. -e'
2. -esz
3. -e
4. -emy
5. -ecie
6. -a'

4. -my
5. -icie
6. -a'

The Past Tense:

In the past tense, the verb byc' (to be) behaves as a regular verb; the regular endings are added to the stem.

The endings for the masculine gender are:

1. -l'em
2. -l'es'
3. -l'
4. -lis'my
5. -lis'cie
6. -li

The endings for the feminine gender are:

1. -l'am
2. -l'as'
3. -l'a
4. -l'ys'my
5. -l'ys'cie
6. -l'y

Things in the Neuter gender cannot speak for themselves, nor can a sane person speak to something that is of the Neuter gender, so only the 3rd person singular *s/he/it* and the 3rd person plural *They* endings exist for the Neuter gender:

3. -l'o
6. -l'y



The Future Tense:

For the future tense, one must use the future tense form of byc' (to be) and then say the verb which they are intending to say that will happen.

1. be'de'
2. be'dziesz
3. be'dzie
4. be'dziemy
5. be'dziecie
6. be'da'

For instance, if one wanted to say that [[something]] will work, they could say...

Using the infinitive:

1. be'de' pracowac'
2. be'dziesz pracowac'
3. be'dzie pracowac'
4. be'dziemy pracowac'
5. be'dziecie pracowac'
6. be'da' pracowac'

If the Direct Object [[Item receiving the action]] is Masculine:

1. be'de' pracowal'
2. be'dziesz pracowal'
3. be'dzie pracowal'
4. be'dziemy pracowali
5. be'dziecie pracowali
6. be'da' pracowali

If the Direct Object is Feminine:

1. be'de' pracowal'a
2. be'dziesz pracowal'a
3. be'dzie pracowal'a
4. be'dziemy pracowal'y
5. be'dziecie pracowal'y
6. be'da' pracowal'y

If the Direct Object is Neuter:

3. be'dzie pracowal'o
6. be'da' pracowal'y


To use the future tense, it would be equally valid to use the infinitive as it would be to use the other options.

Verb moods:

There are three basic moods in Polish: Indicative, Imperative, and Conditional.

In the Indicative mood, we inform somebody about something. The information can also take on the form of a question or a negative sentence. The indicative mood is the only mood described in the posts above.

In the Imperative mood, we tell someone to do something: it only occurs in the 2nd person singular, 1st person plural, and 2nd person plural.

2. Pisz! (Write! *Directed at one person*)
4. Piszmy! (Let us write! *Directed at group of people, in which the speaker is a part of*)
5. Piszcie! (Write! *Directed at a group of people, in which the speaker is not a part of*)

The imperative mood can also have various levels of intensity- Saying Prosze', followed by an infinitive, is a request rather than an order. *Prosze Pisac' (Please write)* Masz (You are to), musisz (You must), trzeba (It is necessary to), or powinienes' (You should) can mean different things.


In the Condidional mood, we say that an action is imaginative, or is wanted and possible.

1. -bym
2. -bys'
3. -by
4. -bys'my
5. -bys'cie
6. -by

For instance;

If the Direct Object is Masculine:

1. chcial'bym (I would want; I would like)
2. chcial'bys' (You would want; You would like)
3. chcial'by
4. chcielibys'my
5. chcielibys'cie
6. chcieliby

If the Direct Object is Feminine:

1. chcial'abym (I would want; I would like)
2. chcial'abys' (You would want; You would like)
3. chcial'aby
4. chcial'ybys'my
5. chcial'ybys'cie
6. chcial'yby

If the Direct Object is Neuter:

3. chcial'oby
6. chcial'yby


In addition, -by can come before the verb, making them two seperate words: e.g. Ja bym chcial'

This section will describe words that may seem to be small, but they are not uncommon- They are indispensable.

Prepositions:

w (inside)
na (on the surface)
pod (below; underneath)
nad (above)
za (behind)
przed (in front of)
kol'o (near; next to)
przy (near; next to)
mie'dzy (between two objects)
ws'ro'd (amongst; inside a homogenous group)


Conjunctions:

Connective: i (and), oraz (and), tez' (also), takz'e (), ro'wniez' (equally), zaro'wno (equally), and nie tylko (not only)

Contrasting: ale (but), lecz (however), and natomiast (yet).

Conclusive: Wie'c (thus), zatem (in conclusion), and dlatego (because of this).

Alternative: albo (or), or lub (or).


Clauses:

Casual: bo (because), poniewaz' (because), dlatego z'e (because of)

Real conditional: jes'li (if), jez'eli (if), jak (if).

Unreal conditional: gdyby (if *used in situations in which the following verb is unlikely to happen*)

object and aim: z'eby, aby, by (in order to...*E.G. the thread, "aaaaby zostac modem" (In order to become a mod)*)

Time: kiedy (when), gdy (when), az' (until), po'ki [POOkee] (until), dopo'ki (until), zanim (before).

Diminutives!

One with a keen eye will easily notice that Poles love diminutives.

As some examples...

Herbata - Herbatka (Tea)
Kawa - Kawka (Coffee)
Minuta - Minutka (Minute)
L'awka - L'aweczka (Bench)
Bul'ka - Bul'eczka (Bread roll)
Ksia'z'ka - Ksia'z'eczka (Book)
Dom - Domek (House)
Kwiat - Kwiatek (Flower)
Las - Lasek (Woods; Forest)
Pies - Piesek (Dog)
Spacer - Spacerek (Noun: walk; stroll)
Balon - Balonik (Baloon)
Sto'l [stOO]- Stolik [sTOLeek] (Table)
Wazon - Wazonik (Vase)
Klucz - Kluczyk (Key)
Teatr [TEatr] - Teatrzyk [teATjik]


In addition, we can make a diminitive even sweeter by using a double equivalent of the suffix -ek, namely -eczek:

Dom - Domek - Domeczek (House)
Las - Lasek - Laseczek (Woods; Forest)
Zegar [ZEgar] - Zegarek [zeGARek] - Zegareczek [zegarECHek] (Noun: Watch *on your hand*)
Kon' [KON~] - Konik [KONeek] - Konieszek [konOOshek] (Horse)
Kwiat - Kwiatek - Kwiatuszek (Flower)
Palec - Paluszek (Finger)


Neuter nounts can take the diminutive suffix -ko:

Jajo - Jajko (Egg)
Piwo - Piwko (Beer)
Wino - Winko (Wine)

From here, we can double he -ko into -eczko:

Jajo - Jajko - Jajeczko (Egg)
Piwo - Piwko - Piweczko (Beer)
Wino - Winko - Wineczko (Wine *Wineczko is a valid word, but sounds so absurd my spell-checker marks it as incorrect*)


Surprisingly, there are also diminutives for adjectives:

Bial'y - Bielutki (White)
Mal'y - Malutki (Small)
Czysty - Czys'ciutki (Clean)
Lekki - Leciutki (Soft; Light)

Typically, as you an see, the ending is -utki, but there is one more derived from the Ukranian language, -en'ki:

mal'y [MAwih] - malutki [maLOOTkee] - malen'ki [maLEN~kee] (Small)

Diminutives may also have a commercial value; to attract customers by putting them in a better mood. Casiers prefer saying Pienia'z'ki instead of Pienia'dze (money), etc.

Commonly used/useful phrases

Greetings:

Hi! - Czes'c' [CHEsch]
Welcome! - Witam [VEEtam]
I invite you - Zapraszam [zaPRASHam]
We invite you - Zapraszamy [zaPRASHami]
Good day - Dzien' dobry [JEN DOBri]
How's it going? - Jak lecie? [YAK LEchee?]
What's new? - Co nowego? [CHO noVEgo?]
Hey! - Hej! [HEY!]
Nice to see you - Mil'o cie widziec' [MEEwo CHE VEEJetch]
I like your tie - Lubie twoj krawat [LOObee-eh tfooy KRAvat]

Farewells:

Bye (informal) - czes'c' [CHEsch]
Bye-bye - pa-pa [pah-PAH]
Goodbye - Z'egnam [JEGnam]
Goodbye *from a group* - Z'egnamy [JEGnamih]
{Goodbye} for now (informal) - Na razie [naRAje]
That's not the exit; that's the wine cellar! - To nie wyjs'cie, to jest piwnica na wino! [TO nye yest VIYSche, to yest PEEVneecha na VEEno!]
There goes our 1890 Bordeaux. - I juz nie ma naszego 1890 Bordeaux. [EE YOOSH nye ma nashEgo TYSHonch OSHEMset jevenJEshunt borDOW.]

Practical use:

Where's the bathroom? - Gdzie jest ubikacja? [gJE yest oobeeCACHia?]
Where's the currency exchange? - Gdzie jest kantor? [gJE yest KANtor?]
How much does this cost? - Ile to kosztuje? [EEleh TO KOSHtooye?]
That's too much- can we bargain? - To za duz'o- moz'emy targowac'? [TO za DOOjo- mojEMih sheh tarGOVach?]
